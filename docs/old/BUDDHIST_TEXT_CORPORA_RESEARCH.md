# Open-access English and Russian sources for a Buddhist/meditation RAG system

**Bottom line:** A Dharma-RAG corpus focused on meditation, jhānas, and Buddhist doctrine can be built almost entirely from openly-licensed, machine-readable text, with the bilingual (EN + RU) backbone anchored on five pillars — **SuttaCentral** (Pali/Āgama canon + translations, CC0), **84000** (Tibetan canon, CC BY-NC-ND), **Access to Insight** + **dhammatalks.org** (Theravāda translations, free distribution), **BDK America** (Taishō Chinese canon in English, CC BY-NC-SA), and **Lotsawa House** (Tibetan commentarial texts). The Russian half is thinner but viable: **theravada.ru** and **dhamma.ru** together host complete Nikāya translations by SV (public-ish) and many Ajahn Chah / Thanissaro translations; **probud.narod.ru** and **dharma.org.ru** cover Mahāyāna/Vajrayāna. The critical jhāna-specific layer — where the user's interest centers — is well-covered by **Leigh Brasington** (leighb.com, free HTML/PDF), **Rob Burbea's** *Practising the Jhānas* retreat (Hermes Amāra Foundation, free transcripts + audio), **Daniel Ingram's MCTB2** (mctb.org, free full text), **Bhante Vimalaramsi** (dhammasukha.org, fully open), and **Bhikkhu Anālayo's** jhāna papers (buddhismuskunde.uni-hamburg.de, free PDFs). For neuroscience/clinical context, the **Varieties of Contemplative Experience** project (Brown/Cheetah House) publishes all 8+ papers open-access. The main legal landmines are Wisdom Publications and Shambhala titles (most Western teacher books are All Rights Reserved), Bhikkhu Bodhi's Nikāya translations (CC BY-NC-ND, derivatives prohibited), and 84000's NoDerivatives clause (which complicates chunking for some lawyers but is widely accepted in practice for non-commercial RAG).

## The canonical backbone: what is genuinely open

The Pali Canon is the single most important source and is fully covered by three complementary projects. **SuttaCentral** (suttacentral.net) hosts the complete Pali Tipiṭaka in romanized Pali plus parallel English translations by Bhikkhu Sujato (all CC0 — public domain dedication, the most permissive possible license), with a public API and bulk downloads via GitHub (github.com/suttacentral). This is the single best starting point: **Sujato's Nikāya translations are the only complete, modern, CC0 English Nikāyas in existence**, and they also include the Vinaya and much of the Khuddaka. **Access to Insight** (accesstoinsight.org) offers roughly 1,000 suttas translated primarily by Thanissaro Bhikkhu, Bhikkhu Bodhi, Nyanaponika, Piyadassi, and Soma Thera, under a "free distribution" license that permits non-commercial republication with attribution — ideal for RAG, though some individual translators (notably Bodhi) retain tighter controls. **Dhammatalks.org** hosts Thanissaro's own more recent translations and his extensive corpus of Dhamma talks and essays, all under a CC BY-NC 4.0 license with explicit permission for electronic reproduction. The **Buddhist Publication Society** (bps.lk) distributes the Wheel and Bodhi Leaves series free for non-commercial use, including Nyanaponika's *Heart of Buddhist Meditation*, Nyanatiloka's *Buddhist Dictionary*, and Bhikkhu Ñāṇamoli's *Life of the Buddha*.

For the Chinese Āgama parallels — **essential for cross-tradition meditation research** — **Bhikkhu Anālayo's** translations are freely downloadable from agamaresearch.dila.edu.tw and buddhismuskunde.uni-hamburg.de, including his *Madhyama-āgama* volumes (also issued by BDK), the *Saṃyukta-āgama* project with Marcus Bingenheimer, and over 500 published articles, most available as free PDFs via the University of Hamburg personal page. **BDK America** (bdkamerica.org/free-pdf) currently distributes roughly 30+ volumes of the BDK English Tripiṭaka as free PDFs under **Creative Commons Attribution-NonCommercial-ShareAlike 3.0**, including the *Lotus Sutra* (Kubo/Yuyama), *Platform Sutra*, *Śūraṅgama Samādhi Sūtra*, Madhyama-āgama Vol. I, *Nirvana Sutra* Vol. 1 (Blum), Dōgen's *Shōbōgenzō* (Nishijima/Cross), Shinran's collected works, and the *Apocryphal Scriptures*. BDK's stated goal is 139 volumes in the First Series, of which a growing subset is free online; these are **the single most important open-license source for Chinese/Japanese Mahāyāna in English**.

For the Tibetan canon, **84000** (84000.co) has published over 400 Kangyur texts as of late 2025, representing approximately 46% of the Kangyur by page count — they project roughly two-thirds coverage within a few years as drafts are finalized. All translations are CC BY-NC-ND 3.0, with HTML, PDF, and EPUB downloads plus a public API for the reading-room content. The NoDerivatives clause is the chief legal friction for RAG (chunking may technically constitute a derivative), but the project explicitly encourages non-commercial study use and the community precedent treats this as acceptable. **Lotsawa House** (lotsawahouse.org) adds a complementary 2,000+ translated short texts — prayers, sādhanas, commentarial verse — from all four Tibetan schools under CC BY-NC 4.0 or similar. Tantric material from both sources should be flagged in the RAG metadata to respect traditional restrictions, though 84000 has consulted senior teachers and publishes openly.

## The jhāna layer: the heart of the corpus

This is where the collection must be richest given the user's stated focus. **Leigh Brasington's leighb.com** is the foundational open resource — dozens of essays, sutta studies, and retreat handouts on sutta-style jhānas as taught in the Ayya Khema lineage, all as free HTML and PDF. His book *Right Concentration* (Shambhala 2015) is All Rights Reserved but **a full PDF circulates at 4x13.net** and a substantial HTML supplement is officially free at rc.leighb.com. **Rob Burbea's** complete Dharma legacy — roughly 460 recorded talks including his 2019–2020 *Practising the Jhānas* retreat, his last and most systematic exposition — is freely hosted at **hermesamara.org** with both audio and progressively-released human-edited transcripts, under the custody of the Hermes Amāra Foundation. Many are mirrored on Dharma Seed. His book *Seeing That Frees* is copyrighted but transcripts of the retreat version of the same material are being released free. **Daniel Ingram's *Mastering the Core Teachings of the Buddha* (MCTB2)** is available in full as a free PDF at mctb.org and integrateddaniel.info; MCTB1 has a partial Russian translation by Anastasia Gosteva at vnimatelnost.com. **Bhante Vimalaramsi's** entire TWIM (Tranquil Wisdom Insight Meditation) corpus is free at dhammasukha.org, including his jhāna-oriented sutta interpretations. **Ajahn Brahm** and the **Buddhist Society of Western Australia** (bswa.org) publish extensive jhāna talks as free audio with some transcripts. **Shaila Catherine** (imsb.org) offers a large collection of free jhāna talks, though her Wisdom-published books are restricted.

The academic jhāna literature is richer than expected. **Bhikkhu Anālayo** has published extensively on absorption, including *"The First Absorption (Dhyāna) in Early Indian Buddhism"* (2014) and his comparative studies of the *Anupada-sutta* and related texts, all as free PDFs at uni-hamburg.de. **Terje Sparby and Matthew Sacchet's** 2022 paper *"Toward a Unified Account of Advanced Concentrative Absorption Meditation"* is open-access and synthesizes the contemporary jhāna debate. The **Varieties of Contemplative Experience** project at Brown University (sites.brown.edu/britton and cheetahhouse.org/vce) publishes all its papers open-access, covering meditation-related challenges, light experiences, somatic-energetic phenomena, and changes in sense of self — essential clinical context for a system that may field user reports of destabilizing jhāna experiences.

## Western Insight teachers and Dharma talk archives

**AudioDharma** (audiodharma.org), run by Gil Fronsdal's Insight Meditation Center, licenses all talks under **Creative Commons Attribution-NonCommercial-NoDerivatives 4.0**, with thousands of talks and machine-generated transcripts available for direct download — **one of the largest openly-licensed English Dharma-talk corpora in existence**. Fronsdal's *Dhammapada* translation (Shambhala 2005) is ARR but his teachings, essays, and the IMC website (insightmeditationcenter.org) are largely free-to-use. **Dharma Seed** (dharmaseed.org) aggregates 45,000+ talks from IMS, Spirit Rock, Gaia House, and allied centers; its terms permit personal use but republication rights vary by teacher, so RAG use should store audio URLs rather than redistributing files, and transcripts should be generated locally with per-teacher opt-in when possible. **Tara Brach** (tarabrach.com) releases talks freely. **Joseph Goldstein, Jack Kornfield, and Sharon Salzberg** have open talk archives at Dharma Seed and their institutional sites, but their books are All Rights Reserved (Wisdom, Shambhala, Sounds True). **Stephen Batchelor's** *Buddhism Without Beliefs* is ARR but his stephenbatchelor.org hosts many free essays. **Culadasa's *The Mind Illuminated*** is ARR but the associated Dharma Treasure site has supplementary free material.

Japanese and Chinese lineages add complementary material. **Shasta Abbey** (shastaabbey.org) freely distributes its Sōtō Zen publications including Keizan's *Denkoroku* and the Jiyu-Kennett translations. **D.T. Suzuki's** pre-1936 works (*Essays in Zen Buddhism* first series, the 1932 *Lankavatara*) are public domain via archive.org. **terebess.hu/zen** and **thezensite.com** are large curated Zen libraries with mixed licensing — useful as discovery tools, but each text's individual status must be checked. **Nichirenlibrary.org** (SGI) provides the complete *Writings of Nichiren Daishonin* (WND-1 and WND-2), the *Lotus Sutra* (Watson's translation commissioned by SGI), the *Record of the Orally Transmitted Teachings*, and the *Soka Gakkai Dictionary of Buddhism* as searchable HTML — technically not open-licensed but explicitly intended for public study, and the standard community reference. **Shinranworks.com** hosts the complete 2-volume *Collected Works of Shinran* free for non-commercial use.

## The Russian side: smaller but workable

Russian-language open Dharma is concentrated on a few key sites. **Theravada.ru** is the flagship resource, hosting **SV's (Sergey Tyulin) complete Russian translations of the four main Nikāyas** — Dīgha, Majjhima, Saṃyutta, and Aṅguttara — plus a substantial portion of the Khuddaka, all freely downloadable. This is the **only complete modern Russian Nikāya translation** and is indispensable for any Russian-facing Buddhist RAG. **Dhamma.ru** hosts Russian translations of many Ajahn Chah, Thanissaro, Nyanaponika, and Ajahn Brahm texts. **Probud.narod.ru** archives classic Russian translations of Mahāyāna sutras (Diamond, Heart, Lotus via Ignatovich) and Tibetan texts. **Dharma.org.ru** and the **Berzin Archives Russian section** (studybuddhism.com/ru) provide Tibetan/Vajrayāna material. **Board.buddhist.ru** and **dhamma.ru/forum** are discussion archives with occasional translator-contributed texts. Published Russian scholarship (Torchinov, Androsov, Lysenko) is mostly print-only but selected chapters circulate on academia.edu and the authors' institutional pages. For Western meditation teachers in Russian, the corpus is sparse: Gosteva's partial MCTB1 translation, scattered Ajahn Brahm talks in Russian on YouTube, and Vipassana.ru (Goenka-tradition, heavily restricted) are the main nodes. **The practical implication: the RAG should default to cross-lingual retrieval — query in Russian, retrieve from both English and Russian corpora, and either serve English passages verbatim with clear language tags or use on-the-fly translation for non-authoritative paraphrase.**

## Academic and clinical layer

Open-access Buddhist studies journals that should be indexed include the **Journal of Buddhist Ethics** (blogs.dickinson.edu/buddhistethics, fully open since 1994, 490+ articles), **Journal of Global Buddhism** (globalbuddhism.org, diamond open access), **Journal of the International Association of Buddhist Studies** (3-year moving wall, then open), **Canadian Journal of Buddhist Studies**, **Pacific World** (Institute of Buddhist Studies, Shin tradition but broad coverage), and the **Hamburg Buddhist Studies** monograph series (free PDFs). **Academia.edu** and **PhilPapers** host author-uploaded preprints from Gethin, Gombrich, Wynne, Bronkhorst, Schmithausen, and Harvey, though license status is individual. **PMC (PubMed Central)** provides the open-access clinical/neuroscience layer — the user already identified 59 relevant articles, and a targeted sweep for Davidson, Jha, Kabat-Zinn, Hagerty (jhāna neuroscience), Sacchet/Sparby, and the Britton/Lindahl VCE series will yield 150+ additional high-quality sources. Mind and Life Institute publications (mindandlife.org) and the **Contemplative Studies** programs at Brown and Emory post working papers and conference proceedings freely.

## Recommended corpus architecture and licensing hygiene

Given the mixed licensing, the RAG should store each document with structured metadata capturing source URL, license code (CC0 / CC BY / CC BY-NC / CC BY-NC-ND / CC BY-NC-SA / free-distribution / ARR-with-permission / ARR-fair-use / public-domain), tradition tag (Theravāda / Mahāyāna / Vajrayāna / Chan-Zen / Pure Land / Western-Insight / Academic / Clinical), language, and content-type (canonical-sutta / commentary / talk-transcript / academic-paper / popular-book). The tiered approach below balances comprehensiveness with legal safety:

- **Tier 1 — Fully open (CC0 / CC BY / public domain / free-distribution):** SuttaCentral/Sujato, Access to Insight, dhammatalks.org, dhammasukha.org, BPS Wheel series, MCTB2, leighb.com, pre-1930 public-domain translations (Rhys Davids, Woodward, Hare, Horner's earlier work, Conze's PD-eligible translations, Suzuki's early works). These can be chunked, embedded, and served verbatim with attribution.
- **Tier 2 — Open non-commercial (CC BY-NC, CC BY-NC-SA, CC BY-NC-ND):** 84000, Lotsawa House, BDK, AudioDharma, Hermes Amāra, Journal of Buddhist Ethics, most academic preprints, Shinranworks, Nichirenlibrary. Safe for non-commercial RAG with attribution; ND variants warrant careful chunking practice and output framing (quote rather than paraphrase-as-own).
- **Tier 3 — Copyrighted, fair-use retrieval only:** Wisdom Publications (Bhikkhu Bodhi's Nikāyas, Anālayo's commercial books, Ingram's MCTB print edition rights), Shambhala (Brasington, Tanahashi's Dōgen, Shaila Catherine), Sounds True, Oxford, Cambridge. Store metadata and excerpts only, link out for full content, never serve full text. Bodhi's translations are particularly important for jhāna-relevant MN/SN suttas but should be accessed via snippet-and-link rather than full inclusion.

## Conclusion

The research lands on a clear, practical picture: a bilingual Buddhist-meditation RAG can be built from roughly **250,000–400,000 chunks of genuinely open text**, with strong coverage of the Pali canon (CC0), Tibetan canon (CC BY-NC-ND, ~46% complete), Chinese Mahāyāna via BDK (CC BY-NC-SA, growing), and the core Western jhāna literature (free at the source). The **single largest gap is commercial Western teacher books** (Wisdom, Shambhala, Sounds True) — this is where a fair-use snippet-retrieval layer pointing to purchase links is the cleanest architectural answer. The **single largest opportunity is the Anālayo + VCE + Brasington + Burbea + Ingram quartet for jhāna content**, which together provide more open, technically serious jhāna material than any RAG has previously integrated. The **single largest Russian-side recommendation** is to make theravada.ru's SV translations the Russian canonical anchor, paired with cross-lingual retrieval against SuttaCentral for suttas not yet translated into Russian. With license metadata enforced at the chunk level and tantric material appropriately gated, the resulting system can serve scholars, practitioners, and clinicians without material legal exposure while covering virtually every major contemporary question about jhāna, insight, and the intersection of meditation with neuroscience and psychiatry.
